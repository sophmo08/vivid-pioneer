abstract: 'Do migrant apprentices show more absenteeism or lower engagement than their non-migrant peers — and what workplace conditions influence these outcomes? Drawing on longitudinal data from over 600 apprentices in nearly 200 German organizations, this study examines how individual and organizational factors shape training experiences and workplace behavior. summary: 'Do apprentices with a migration background show more absenteeism during their training? And are they less engaged at work? These questions often come up in public debates about vocational training and labour market integration, yet many assumptions are rarely examined empirically. Drawing on data from the Integration@Work project at the Cluster of Excellence "The Politics of Inequality", this study follows apprentices across a large number of German companies. Using survey data from over 600 apprentices in nearly 200 organizations, the findings paint a more nuanced picture than common stereotypes suggest. Apprentices with a migration background report higher levels of organizational engagement. They also report more absenteeism, but this decreases dramatically when they feel supported by supervisors and colleagues. Structured onboarding processes can further strengthen engagement. Instead of focusing primarily on presumed deficits such as language skills or school grades, organizations can make a real difference by strengthening supportive workplace environments — particularly through stronger social support by supervisors and colleagues, inclusive onboarding processes, and fostering a workplace climate where apprentices feel supported and integrated.'
