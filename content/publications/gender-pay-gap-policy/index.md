abstract: 'In 2023, the gender pay gap in Germany stood at 18 percent — unchanged for the fifth consecutive year, despite women increasingly entering well-paid occupations. Drawing on salary data from 1,780,008 employees across 152,717 companies, this policy paper analyzes how individual, organizational, and political factors contribute to persistent wage inequality between men and women, and derives concrete recommendations for employees, companies, and policymakers.'
summary: 'Why does the gender pay gap in Germany persist at 18 percent — even as women increasingly work in well-paid jobs and the same occupations as men? Drawing on salary data from over 1.7 million employees across Germany, this policy paper identifies four key mechanisms. At the individual level, men are rewarded more financially for the same amount of work experience and leadership responsibility than women — a gap that widens over the course of a career. At the organizational level, companies with on-site childcare show a meaningfully smaller pay gap, while token female representation in top management alone does not reduce wage inequality. At the political level, supervisory board gender quotas only reduce pay gaps once full parity of 50 percent is reached — the current 30 percent threshold has no measurable effect. Finally, strong regional variation — with West Germany showing a gap nearly 45 percent higher than some East German states — points to the role of childcare infrastructure and political context. The paper recommends binding pay policies, family-friendly organizational structures, full gender parity on supervisory boards, and expanded childcare provision as concrete levers for closing the gap.'
